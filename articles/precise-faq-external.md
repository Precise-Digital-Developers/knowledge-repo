## What is music distribution?

Music distribution is the process of getting recorded music to the many different digital stores — commonly called Digital Service Providers, or DSPs — around the globe. This includes Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, TikTok, Pandora, Amazon Music, Facebook/Instagram and many more.

Precise directly licenses and distributes to almost all key platforms. Our direct relationships with the major stores help us better support artists and surface opportunities that wouldn't otherwise reach independents. Beyond delivery, we take a hands-on approach with partners — optimising catalogues, syncing lyrics, creating visual material and more.

Please note: Precise is **not** a public self-serve / DIY distribution platform. We work selectively with artists and partners we can properly support as an engaged, hands-on partner.

### What platforms does Precise distribute to?

Spotify, TikTok, Apple Music, Vevo, YouTube Music, Pandora, Amazon Music, SoundCloud, Deezer, Facebook/Instagram, iHeartRadio, Peloton, Boomplay, AWA, Audiomack, Tidal, JioSaavn, Tencent, TikTok Music, Resso, ACRCloud, Audible Magic, Flo, Mixcloud, KKBox, JOOX, Anghami, and others.

## Release basics

### What assets do I need to provide to distribute a release?

#### Artwork

- High-resolution cover art, preferably 3000×3000 pixels.
- Artwork must accurately reflect the artist name and song title. If you have multiple primary artists, credit them as `Artist A & Artist B` or `Artist A x Artist B` rather than `feat.` — DSPs are strict about this.

#### Audio

- Master-quality audio files, ideally `.wav` or `.flac`. MP3 is not accepted.

#### Metadata

- Song title, artist name, composer / writer credits, and any other relevant credits.
- Your desired release date.
- If the release was previously distributed elsewhere, include the original ISRC and UPC codes assigned by the previous distributor. If it's your first release, Precise can assign new ISRC and UPC codes on your behalf.

### How long does delivery take?

Typically your music will go live on most platforms within 3 days of submission — sometimes sooner. YouTube Music, Apple Music, TikTok and Facebook are usually the fastest. Precise aims to deliver submitted releases to DSPs within 12 hours of receiving them.

For optimal results, upload at least two to two-and-a-half weeks before your desired release date. This gives editorial teams at the streaming platforms enough time to review your song for playlist placement.

### When should I submit a release?

We recommend submitting at least two weeks (14 days) before your desired release date. At minimum, we require releases to be submitted within five business days (UTC) of the desired release date.

### Timed releases and region-specific timing

The Precise dashboard lets you set your release date and track delivery progress. Our team verifies metadata, ISRCs, and artwork before approval. If you have region-specific timing (for example, an NZ-first release), Precise can adjust delivery accordingly.

## Royalty payments

### How does my music make money?

Most platforms calculate royalties differently, and rates vary based on a number of factors:

- Whether the listener is a paid subscriber or a free/ad-supported user.
- The country the stream originates from — Australia, New Zealand and the United States typically have higher rates than other markets.
- Whether an ad was served before or during playback.
- How many times a listener streamed the song.

For deeper explainers:

- [Spotify: Loud and Clear](https://loudandclear.byspotify.com/)
- [Apple Music Insights: Royalties](https://artists.apple.com/support/1124-apple-music-insights-royalty-rate)
- [Dissecting the Digital Dollar](https://themmf.net/digitaldollar/)

### How long before I see royalties from a new release?

Precise is typically paid by DSPs **two months after** the use of your music. Once we receive payment, we pass royalties on to you the following month. So if your song is played on Spotify in January: Spotify pays Precise in March, and you receive payment from us by the end of April.

Expect to see your first royalty payment roughly three months after your release date. Clients moving a catalogue over from another distributor (such as TuneCore or DistroKid) should expect the same three-month delay after their previous distributor hands off.

### Why didn't I receive the full amount shown on my statement?

International transactions have two cost components — one on our side and one on yours:

- Precise covers a fixed transfer fee on our side.
- Your receiving bank may charge a processing fee for incoming international payments. We can't cover fees on the artist's side; that's set by your local bank.

Currency conversion also matters. If your statement shows an NZD total but your account receives AUD, USD, GBP or EUR, the amount you see will depend on the exchange rate at the time of transfer. A statement showing NZD $1,000 might land as roughly NZD $923-equivalent in your local currency before any bank fees on your end.

### What is artificial streaming?

Third parties that promise playlist placements or guaranteed streams in exchange for payment are almost always using illegitimate practices. These services can result in withheld royalties, flagged streams, or complete removal of your catalogue from streaming services.

DSPs work hard to ensure streams reflect genuine user listening intent. If a service determines that you (or a third party acting on your behalf) have boosted play counts through automated, deceptive or fraudulent means — bots, click farms, paid playlist placement — they may permanently remove your entire catalogue.

Further reading:

- [Spotify: Artificial streaming](https://artists.spotify.com/artificial-streaming)
- [Spotify: Third-party services that guarantee streams](https://support.spotify.com/nz/artists/article/third-party-services-that-guarantee-streams/)

Precise investigates any music or clients receiving artificial streams, up to and including terminating releases. Any penalties charged by Spotify to Precise will not impact artists who did not release the violating tracks.

## Splits

A splits arrangement is how royalties get divided between collaborators on a track — the main artist, a featured artist, a producer, and so on. Each recipient's share is set as a percentage, and the total must equal 100%.

### Who can receive a split?

Precise's splits policy exists to keep this feature sustainable. Splits are only offered to participants credited on a release as:

- **Producer**
- **Main** or **Featured** artist

Other credit types are generally not set up as direct split recipients. Every split recipient requires a separate contract and a separate bank transfer each pay cycle, which means the admin cost of splitting a release many ways quickly becomes untenable.

### Confirming a split

When submitting split information, make sure:

- The split total equals 100%.
- All parties receiving a split are CC'd on the communication so everyone has a shared record of the arrangement.

## Publishing

Music publishing is the business of managing and promoting musical compositions — the songs themselves, as distinct from any particular recording of them — and the royalties they generate. It covers protecting and licensing musical works, securing performance and mechanical royalties, and making sure songwriters get paid for their work.

Precise can manage your songwriting royalties, collecting from every source possible globally and registering your songs with Performance Rights Organisations (PROs such as APRA AMCOS or ASCAP) to maximise earnings. Precise publishing is offered to individual songwriters — you don't have to be a distribution client to work with us as a publisher.

### How do I become a publishing client with Precise?

As with most publishers, Precise will reach out directly to individuals we wish to sign. We're always open to a conversation with anyone interested in publishing and how it works.

### What are the benefits of a Precise publishing arrangement?

At its most basic, we take over the administrative work of registering your songs with your PRO and ensuring royalties flow through correctly.

Precise Digital has a direct publisher membership with APRA AMCOS covering Australia, New Zealand and the Pacific. We also collect publishing royalties directly from the US via memberships with ASCAP, The MLC, and Music Reports. We use Regalias Digitales as a third-party partner to collect YouTube sync royalties derived from US territories.

Publishing royalties are paid quarterly — March, June, September, and December.

### Do I need to join APRA or another PRO?

Yes. Whatever territory you're based in, there will be a corresponding PRO, and it's important you become a member as an individual songwriter. Those organisations collect royalties for songwriters that cannot be collected by a publisher directly.

For Australia and New Zealand, APRA AMCOS is the organisation to join. In the US, we recommend ASCAP. Most other territories have their own local PRO.

### Mechanicals vs performance rights

They're different:

**Mechanical royalties** are payments made to songwriters and publishers when a song is **reproduced** — whether physically or digitally. The term comes from the early days of the music industry, when songs were mechanically reproduced on piano rolls and phonographs. Mechanicals are paid when:

- A song is streamed on Spotify, Apple Music, etc.
- A song is downloaded from iTunes or similar.
- A song is pressed to CD or vinyl.

**Performance royalties** are paid when a work is publicly performed — broadcast on radio or TV, streamed, or played live.

### Licensing and synchronisation

Publishers grant licenses for various uses of a song, such as in films, TV shows, advertisements, or as a cover version.

**Synchronisation** (or "sync") is when a song is embedded into another form of media — typically an audiovisual piece such as a scene in a TV show, film, or video game. Precise is increasingly active in this space and is building a network of contacts locally and globally with music supervisors.

## Neighbouring rights

The term "neighbouring rights" (sometimes "related rights") refers to royalties generated from the public performance, communication or broadcast of **sound recordings** within a territory. They're "neighbouring" or "related" to the same usage of the underlying musical work (which generates publishing royalties instead).

Usage differs country by country, but some examples of sound recording uses collected by CMOs around the world:

- **Radio** — a sound recording is broadcast on radio.
- **Television** — a recording already synced into a programme or advertisement is subsequently broadcast on TV.
- **Public performance** — a recording is played in a public space such as a bar, café, retail shop or gym.
- **Non-interactive digital services** in some territories — radio simulcasts, catch-up TV, or platforms like SiriusXM in North America.

It's not practical for individual artists to license and collect royalties for every instance of these uses, which is why **Collective Management Organisations (CMOs)** exist in most territories.

Examples include Recorded Music NZ (New Zealand), PPCA (Australia), SoundExchange (US) and PPL (UK). CMOs negotiate blanket licences with music users and distribute neighbouring rights royalties to copyright owners where they are registered.

The global neighbouring rights sector is currently worth approximately USD $2.7 billion across roughly 80 countries, and growing.

Precise offers neighbouring rights collection and administration for all partners in New Zealand, the United States, Australia and (from late 2025) the United Kingdom. Via these direct relationships and reciprocal agreements, Precise can also collect neighbouring rights royalties from the rest of the world.

### Performer rights

Performer rights are **different from** performance rights (which relate to the composition, not the sound recording).

Neighbouring rights are not uniform across the world, and vary more widely between countries than songwriter and composer rights do. Whether a particular artist or recording is eligible for payment can depend on:

- Citizenship of featured artists
- Residency at time of recording
- Where the recording took place (if it was in a country that recognises the right)

Depending on the territory, individual performers who do **not** own copyright in the master sound recording may be entitled to receive a share of revenues directly from the relevant CMO — typically on a 50/50 split between sound recording owner and individual performers, outside of New Zealand and Australia.

### How do I become a neighbouring rights client with Precise?

Precise will actively pursue neighbouring rights collections on your behalf as a distribution client by default, provided you have not already registered directly with CMOs yourself.

If an artist or rights holder is directly registered with CMOs such as Recorded Music NZ, PPCA, SoundExchange, etc, Precise will automatically relinquish any claim so those payments continue to come to you directly. Our neighbouring rights service is structured as a backstop if no direct collection is already happening.

## Digital Service Provider (DSP) FAQs

### How can I correct it when my music is delivered to the wrong profile?

Each major platform has a dedicated correction form:

- **Spotify** — [content-relocation.spotify.com](https://content-relocation.spotify.com/)
- **Apple Music** — [correction form](https://airtable.com/appSn7sKMbBk4W8GH/pagC634ATC32HpYOS/form)

For SoundCloud and YouTube Music corrections, contact your account manager at Precise and we'll handle it directly with the platform.

### How should featured artists be credited on a track?

It's up to the artists involved. It can be useful to credit all artists — especially established ones — as "main" artists so the release appears on each of their profiles.

### Main artist vs featured artist — what's the difference?

If an artist is set as a **main** artist, the release appears on their own Spotify profile, and their profile can be used for editorial pitching. All parties should agree to the layout before submission.

### A track is missing from Meta (Instagram / Facebook). What can I do?

Meta operates on US time zones, so songs are often not yet available on the platform on NZ-release day. In the Precise dashboard you can set the release date to the previous day and run a "Meta update" — the song will then become available within about an hour. Remember to change the release date back to its official date and save after doing this.

Meta search also struggles with generic artist names.

### How do I get my songs on playlists?

Submit your track via the [Precise playlist pitching form](https://precise.digital/pitching-form/). Precise pitches every song where the artist or account manager has completed the form with enough information. Incomplete or low-quality pitches won't be pitched — the more detail and context you provide, the better the outcome.

### Spotify for Artists — how do I claim my profile?

Spotify for Artists lets you:

- Claim and manage your artist profile
- Update photos, bio, and social links
- View live streaming stats and audience insights
- See how playlists, regions and engagement are performing
- Submit upcoming music for playlist consideration

Once your first Precise release is live:

1. Go to [artists.spotify.com/claim](https://artists.spotify.com/claim)
2. Log in with your Spotify account (or create one).
3. Search for your artist name and select your profile.
4. Choose "I manage this artist."
5. Follow the prompts — Spotify may ask for verification via linked socials or an official website.

If your music ends up on the wrong artist profile, use Spotify's [relocation form](https://content-relocation.spotify.com/).

### How is music monetised on TikTok?

TikTok doesn't pay out ad-revenue shares based on plays in the same way Spotify does. Royalty payments are based on the number of times a piece of music is used when first creating a video.

### How is music monetised on Facebook / Instagram?

Once music is made available via Facebook Rights Manager, it can be monetised as user content matches against it. Meta operates on a revenue-share basis with rights holders — a portion of ad revenue from eligible videos goes to the rights holder.

You can opt out of monetisation by whitelisting your own profile, allowing content to be used on your own social properties without being matched or monetised.

### Will I receive royalties when my music is used in Instagram Live?

No — Instagram Live is treated as a real-time broadcast, not a monetised music use. No mechanical or performance royalties are triggered automatically by those live streams.

However, you may still earn royalties if the music used in a live stream is later saved, reposted (as a video or story clip), and matched by Meta's Rights Manager system.

### Will I receive royalties when someone uses my music in their post?

Yes — if your music has been officially distributed to Meta through Precise, you'll receive royalties when it's used in someone else's:

- Posts
- Stories
- Reels
- Profile content

Meta's Rights Manager system tracks when your distributed audio is used anywhere across Facebook or Instagram.

You won't receive royalties via Meta if:

- The user uploaded your song manually (e.g., recording it live rather than selecting it from the music library).
- The content was whitelisted (approved for unpaid use on specific profiles).
- Your music was used in a live stream (Instagram Live is not monetised).

### How do I create an artist YouTube channel?

1. **Create the channel.** Sign in to [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com) with a Google account, click your profile picture → "Create a channel," choose "Use a custom name," and enter your artist name exactly as it appears on your releases. Upload a profile image, banner art, and links to your socials. Once live, [verify the channel](https://www.youtube.com/verify) for longer uploads.
2. **Grant Precise access.** Go to YouTube Studio → Settings → Permissions → Invite, and add your Precise YouTube contact as a Manager. This lets Precise monetise videos, upload official music videos (including Vevo), and optimise metadata, thumbnails, and rights management.
3. **Official Artist Channel (OAC) setup.** Once your music is distributed through Precise, your official audio tracks will automatically appear on YouTube Music. Precise can then merge your YouTube channel and YouTube Music profile into one Official Artist Channel.

Benefits of an OAC:

- Combines all your content (music videos, tracks, fan uploads) in one place.
- Displays your catalogue and playlists under a dedicated "Music" tab.
- Makes your channel eligible for YouTube analytics and monetisation tools.
- Improves visibility and credibility as an official artist.

Precise handles the OAC application — you just need an active artist channel, at least one distributed release under your artist name, and Precise added as a Manager.

### Vevo — pros and cons

Vevo is a music video platform partnered with YouTube. Your videos appear on both Vevo and your YouTube channel via a merged Official Artist Channel.

#### Pros

- **Higher credibility.** The Vevo brand reads as premium and official.
- **Monetisation.** Videos often attract higher ad rates due to the premium nature of the content.
- **OAC integration.** Vevo can be merged into your main YouTube channel, giving you one unified presence.
- **Algorithm boost.** Vevo content benefits from stronger YouTube discoverability.
- **Rights protection.** Content is managed under Vevo's rights system, helping with copyright claims and revenue.
- **Playlist placement.** Vevo curates official playlists — genre-specific or trending collections — where music videos can be featured.
- **Multi-platform reach.** Vevo videos are available both on YouTube and via Vevo's own TV app and connected-TV distribution network.

#### Cons

- **No direct uploads.** Artists can't upload or edit content directly — everything goes through a distributor.
- **Slower turnaround.** Publishing and updates take longer due to Vevo's QC process.
- **Not visible in YouTube Studio.** Vevo videos don't show up in your YouTube Studio dashboard for direct editing, though analytics do appear under Content.
- **Less customisation.** Limited control over thumbnails, tags, and metadata without going through your distributor.

## Copyright

Music copyright grants exclusive rights to the creator (or legal owner) of a musical work — a song or composition — and its sound recordings. This includes the right to reproduce, distribute, perform, and create derivative works based on the copyrighted material.

Copyright in music is automatically granted the moment an original work is created and fixed in a tangible medium (for example, when a recording is made).

There are **two distinct types** of copyright in music:

1. **Song copyright.** Protects the original musical composition — melody, lyrics, structure. Sometimes called the "musical work" or "the publishing."
2. **Sound recording copyright.** Protects the specific recorded performance of the song. Sometimes called the "master" or simply "the record."

### The exclusive rights

A copyright owner has the sole right to:

- **Reproduce** — make copies of the work (printing sheet music, recording a version of the song).
- **Distribute** — sell or distribute copies of the work.
- **Publicly perform** — play the work in public.
- **Communicate** — have the work streamed or broadcast (radio, online streaming).
- **Create derivative works** — new works based on the original (cover versions, remixes).

### How long does copyright last?

In New Zealand, copyright in the song lasts for 50 years after the death of the songwriter. In most other Western countries (US, UK, Australia) it's 70 years.

In New Zealand, copyright in the sound recording lasts for 50 years after the year of release. In most other Western countries it's 70 years.

Useful reference: [NZ Copyright Music Map](https://www.recordedmusic.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/2018-09-05-Recorded-Music-NZ-Music-Map-A3.pdf)

### Copyright takedowns on DSPs

Each DSP has its own copyright and takedown process, but they all follow similar principles grounded in the US Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) and equivalent frameworks elsewhere. Since most DSPs are US-owned and operated, DMCA generally applies globally.

The main points of DMCA:

1. **Notice-and-takedown system.** Online service providers are protected from liability when users infringe, provided they operate a notice-and-takedown system that lets copyright owners flag infringing material for removal.
2. **Anti-circumvention.** Legal protections against unauthorised access to copyrighted works (hacking passwords, circumventing encryption).
3. **Copyright management information.** It's unlawful to provide false copyright management information (names of authors, titles of works) or to remove/alter that information.

Platform-specific processes:

- **Spotify** uses a Copyright Claim Form process. When a duplicate claim occurs (e.g., two distributors delivering the same ISRC), Spotify flags the release and may temporarily hide or block the track until ownership is verified. Precise handles these directly with Spotify's label support team.
- **Apple Music / iTunes** requires proof via an ownership declaration or distributor statement. Apple freezes both versions until documentation is verified.
- **YouTube** operates Content ID and copyright claim systems. Claim types include monetisation claims (revenue redirected until resolved), block claims (video/audio unavailable in certain territories), and takedowns (full removal).
- **Meta** operates under the Rights Manager system. When a copyright issue is flagged, Meta automatically restricts monetisation or visibility; artists can request whitelisting for their own content.
- **TikTok** removes or mutes videos if the audio asset is disputed or reported. Infringement notices are handled via TikTok's Rights Management team.

### Common reasons for takedowns

- Uncleared or unlicensed samples/beats
- Fraudulent streaming
- Copyright ownership disputes
- Artificial streaming

When you deliver a release through Precise, you warrant that you have all the necessary permissions and rights. If there's any uncertainty about ownership or clearances, delivery should be withheld until the situation is fully resolved.

## The New Zealand music industry

### What is the NZ Music Commission?

The [New Zealand Music Commission](https://nzmusic.org.nz/) (NZMC) is a government-funded organisation that promotes New Zealand music and supports the growth of NZ music businesses domestically and internationally. Core activities include:

- The [NZ Music Month](https://nzmusic.org.nz/about-us/nz-music-month/) promotion campaign every May
- [Outward Sound](https://nzmusic.org.nz/outward-sound/about-outward-sound/) — international market development fund
- [Musicians Mentoring in Schools Programme](https://nzmusic.org.nz/music-education/musicians-mentoring-in-schools-programme/)
- [Industry Internship Programme](https://nzmusic.org.nz/about-us/industry-internship-programme/)

### What is NZ On Air?

[NZ On Air](https://www.nzonair.govt.nz/funding/music-funding/) (NZoA) is a government funding agency whose primary function is to make sure New Zealand content — music, TV programmes, games — is created and made available to NZ audiences.

NZ On Air music funding supports the recording and promotion of single songs and multi-song projects. Separate funds are available for artists recording songs with te reo Māori lyrics, artists of Pasifika descent, artists who self-identify as Pan-Asian, and artists creating children's music.

Core NZ On Air music funding options:

- **[NZ Music Single](https://www.nzonair.govt.nz/funding/music-funding/new-music-single-funding/)** — up to $11,000 to create, record, release and promote a new single. Dedicated variants exist for:
  - Artists of [Pasifika descent](https://www.nzonair.govt.nz/funding/music-funding/new-music-pasifika-funding/)
  - Artists who self-identify as [Pan-Asian](https://www.nzonair.govt.nz/funding/music-funding/new-music-pan-asian-funding/)
  - Artists with a [children's content](https://www.nzonair.govt.nz/funding/music-funding/new-music-kids-funding/) focus
- **[Waiata Takitahi](https://www.nzonair.govt.nz/funding/music-funding/waiata-takitahi/)** — up to $15,000 for a single song, jointly funded by NZ On Air and Te Māngai Pāho. For artists recording bilingual singles with a minimum of 25% te reo Māori lyrics and general audience appeal.
- **[New Music Project Funding](https://www.nzonair.govt.nz/funding/music-funding/new-music-project-funding/)** — $11,000 to $55,000 for multi-song projects (EPs and albums), with a [kids' variant](https://www.nzonair.govt.nz/funding/music-funding/new-music-project-kids-funding/).

### What is APRA AMCOS?

[APRA AMCOS](https://www.apraamcos.co.nz/) — the Australasian Performing Right Association and Australasian Mechanical Copyright Owners Society — is a PRO and collective licensing organisation based in New Zealand and Australia.

It represents songwriters, composers and music publishers, making sure they're compensated for the use of their music in public performances, broadcasts, and mechanical reproductions (streaming and downloads included).

Core activities:

- Negotiating licences with radio and TV broadcasters for the communication and reproduction of musical works.
- Operating OneMusic NZ, which licenses public performance of music in retail, hospitality (cafés, bars), gyms, and similar environments. OneMusic licenses both the musical work (on behalf of APRA AMCOS members) and the sound recording (on behalf of Recorded Music NZ members).
- Licensing digital music service providers for mechanical reproduction of musical works (via AMCOS).
- Collecting and distributing royalties to songwriter and publisher members.
- Advocating for songwriters' interests domestically and internationally.
- Organising awards such as the annual [Silver Scroll](https://www.apraamcos.co.nz/music-creators/awards/silver-scroll-awards).
- Running songwriting development initiatives including [Songhubs](https://www.apraamcos.co.nz/music-creators/songhubs), development grants, and supporting wider industry initiatives.

### What is Recorded Music NZ?

[Recorded Music NZ](https://www.recordedmusic.co.nz/) is the NZ CMO, trade association, licensing and advocacy body for record labels and artists.

The organisation manages public performance and broadcast licensing when its members' sound recordings are used, distributes those royalties, and promotes NZ music through initiatives like the Aotearoa Music Awards and the Official Aotearoa Music Charts.

Core activities:

- Negotiating licences with broadcasters for the communication and reproduction of sound recordings.
- Collecting and distributing royalties to artist and label members.
- Advocating for NZ sound recording copyright owners domestically and internationally.
- Organising the annual [Aotearoa Music Awards](https://aotearoamusicawards.co.nz/).
- Producing the official [Aotearoa Music Charts](https://aotearoamusiccharts.co.nz/) and certifying Gold and Platinum records.

### How does a single or album get to number one on the Aotearoa Music Charts?

Recorded Music NZ collects data from DSPs and physical retailers in NZ and compiles the NZ charts weekly. The song or album with the most streams/downloads/sales in a given week takes the number-one spot.

Recorded Music NZ produces a range of charts:

- [Top 40 Singles](https://aotearoamusiccharts.co.nz/charts/singles)
- [Top 40 Albums](https://aotearoamusiccharts.co.nz/charts/albums)
- [Top 20 NZ Singles](https://aotearoamusiccharts.co.nz/charts/aotearoa-singles)
- [Top 20 NZ Albums](https://aotearoamusiccharts.co.nz/charts/aotearoa-albums)
- [Top 10 Te Reo Singles](https://aotearoamusiccharts.co.nz/charts/te-reo-singles)
- [Radioscope radio charts](https://www.radioscope.co.nz/) — across multiple genres

Official [chart terms and rules](https://aotearoamusiccharts.co.nz/api/media/file/Chart-Rules-November-2024.pdf) are published by Recorded Music NZ.

### How does a release get certified Gold or Platinum?

Gold and Platinum certifications are awarded once a release hits qualifying thresholds based on chart-eligible retail sales (physical and digital) plus paid stream equivalents.

Current qualifying thresholds:

- **Gold** — 15,000 singles / 7,500 albums
- **Platinum** — 30,000 singles / 15,000 albums

As a rough guide (the ratio is reviewed quarterly), 175 paid streams equal one sale or "stream equivalent" point. So 175 × 15,000 = **2,625,000 streams** of a single in NZ would qualify that single as Gold.

## Common acronyms

**UPC — Universal Product Code.** A 12-digit barcode used to identify and track entire albums or collections of music — singles, EPs, and albums.

**ISRC — International Standard Recording Code.** Provides a means of identifying sound and video recordings. Each 12-digit code is a unique, permanent identifier for a specific single recording, digitally encoded into the sound recording when it's mastered.

**ISWC — International Standard Musical Work Code.** A unique, permanent, internationally-recognised reference number to identify musical works. An ISWC consists of the letter "T" followed by nine digits plus a check digit to protect against allocation errors — for example, `T-034.524.680-1`. An ISWC is assigned by a PRO when the work is registered (APRA AMCOS, ASCAP, etc).

**PRO — Performance Rights Organisation.** A membership organisation which collects royalties on behalf of copyright holders when musical works are performed publicly (live at an event, or in public spaces like cafés, bars, gyms and community centres) or broadcast (radio, TV).

The NZ and Australian PRO is APRA AMCOS. The Fijian PRO is FPRA. The UK PRO is PRS for Music. The US has multiple PROs, including ASCAP and BMI.

PROs often also perform industry lobbying on copyright legislation, provide grants, run community initiatives and upskilling activities, and present awards for songwriting.

**CMO — Collective Management Organisation.** Sometimes interchangeable with a PRO, but generally refers to a national organisation that represents the rights of the **sound recording copyright owner** (as opposed to the songwriter). CMOs are membership organisations that collect royalties when copyrighted sound recordings are used publicly.

The NZ CMO is Recorded Music NZ. The Australian CMO is PPCA. The UK CMO is PPL. The US CMO is SoundExchange. In most countries, CMOs solely license and collect royalties on behalf of sound recording copyright owners — lobbying, grants, charts, and awards are handled by separate entities (for example, ARIA handles those activities in Australia while PPCA focuses on licensing and collection).
